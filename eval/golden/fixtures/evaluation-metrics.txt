Evaluation Metrics for Retrieval

Retrieval quality is measured against a labeled set of questions. For each
question, a human annotator marks which documents or chunks are relevant.
The retrieval system is then asked the same questions, and its top results
are compared to the labels.

Precision at k counts how many of the first k retrieved items are relevant
and divides that count by k. It answers the question of how clean the top
results are. A precision at 5 of 0.6 means three of the first five results
are relevant. Precision at k punishes systems that pad the top of the list
with off-topic items.

Recall at k counts how many of the truly relevant items appeared somewhere
in the first k results and divides that count by the total number of
relevant items for the question. It answers the question of how complete
the retrieval is. Recall at k is the right metric when missing a relevant
document is more painful than seeing an irrelevant one.

Normalized discounted cumulative gain at k, written NDCG at k, also
considers the position of each relevant item. A relevant item near the top
of the list contributes more than the same item further down. NDCG at k
divides the discounted gain of the actual ranking by the discounted gain
of the perfect ranking, which produces a value between zero and one. NDCG
is useful when the order inside the top k matters, which is common when a
language model only reads the first few chunks and ignores the rest.

These three metrics are complementary. A system can have high precision
and low recall when it returns a tiny but accurate top-k. A system can
have high recall and low precision when k is large and the list is padded.
Reporting all three avoids the trap of optimizing one number at the
expense of the others.
