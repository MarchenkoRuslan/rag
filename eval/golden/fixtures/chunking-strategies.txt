Chunking Strategies for RAG

Chunking is the process of splitting a document into smaller pieces before
embedding. Each chunk becomes one vector in the index, so the chunk size
directly controls retrieval granularity. Chunks that are too small lose
context and force the model to stitch fragments together. Chunks that are too
large dilute the embedding because a single vector now represents many topics
at once.

A practical default is paragraph-aware chunking. The text is first split on
blank lines into paragraphs. Paragraphs are then concatenated into a buffer
until the buffer reaches a target size, at which point the buffer is emitted
as one chunk. This keeps natural boundaries and avoids cutting sentences in
half whenever possible.

Long paragraphs that exceed the target size on their own are handled with a
sliding window. The window walks over the paragraph in steps equal to the
target size minus the overlap, and each step emits one chunk. The window
prefers to break on a newline near the end of the chunk so that sentences are
not cut mid-word.

Two configuration values govern the behavior. The chunk size sets the
maximum number of characters per chunk. The chunk overlap sets how many
characters from the end of one chunk are repeated at the start of the next.
The overlap exists so that a fact that straddles a chunk boundary still
appears intact in at least one chunk. The overlap must be strictly less than
the chunk size, otherwise progress between windows would be zero or negative.
A typical starting point is a chunk size of 500 characters with an overlap of
50 characters.
